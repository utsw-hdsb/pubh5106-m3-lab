Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is defined as structural or functional abnormalities of the kidney that persist for more than 3 months.
CKD is classified into five stages based on the glomerular filtration rate (GFR).
Hypertension is one of the leading causes of chronic kidney disease.
Diabetes mellitus is the most common cause of end-stage renal disease worldwide.
Proteinuria is an early marker of kidney damage and predicts disease progression.
ACE inhibitors and ARBs reduce proteinuria and slow CKD progression.
Anemia commonly occurs in CKD due to decreased erythropoietin production.
Metabolic acidosis develops as kidney function declines.
Hyperkalemia is a life-threatening complication of advanced CKD.
Kidney transplantation offers the best long-term outcomes for end-stage renal disease.
Hemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis are renal replacement therapies for end-stage renal disease.
NSAIDs should be avoided in patients with CKD because they can worsen kidney function.
Phosphate binders are used to manage hyperphosphatemia in CKD patients.
Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in patients with CKD.
eGFR is calculated from serum creatinine using the CKD-EPI equation.
Nephrotic syndrome is characterized by proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, and edema.
Polycystic kidney disease is an inherited disorder that leads to progressive kidney enlargement.
Regular monitoring of serum creatinine and eGFR is essential for CKD management.
Dietary protein restriction may slow the progression of CKD.
Blood pressure control with a target below 130/80 mmHg is recommended for CKD patients.
